# Kolot — The Voices: Home
## Detailed Project Description
## IKF Application, Round 1, 2026

---

### ARTISTIC CONCEPT

#### Introduction and Background

In 1997, the legendary American saxophonist Arnie Lawrence founded the International Center for Creative Music (ICCM) in Jerusalem — a pioneering institution initiated at the invitation of Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin, dedicated to bringing together Jewish and Arab musicians through shared improvisation and mutual learning. Among his youngest students was guitarist Dima Gorelik, who at fifteen was already performing alongside future stars of the Israeli jazz scene, including trumpeter Avishai Cohen, pianist Omri Mor, and pianist Yonatan Avishai. Lawrence's philosophy — that music is a universal language capable of dissolving cultural barriers — became the foundation of Gorelik's artistic identity.

Over the following two decades, Gorelik developed what he now calls a professional instinct: the ability to recognise deep structural similarities between seemingly unrelated musical traditions. Performing with musicians from Morocco, India, West Africa, Turkey, Cuba, and across the Middle East, he began to understand that local musical cultures are never truly isolated — they contain embedded elements of a shared global heritage. The blues and Persian music. Polish folk and Moroccan Gnawa. Renaissance counterpoint and Malagasy polyphony. Gregorian chant and Arabic maqam. Tracing these connections became both an artistic method and a personal mission: to create music that makes multiculturalism feel not like something foreign, but like something that feels like Home.

This insight crystallised into a compositional methodology Gorelik calls *soft cultural displacement*: each piece begins in a musical idiom deeply familiar to the listener and, at the moment of greatest comfort and trust, gradually shifts the frame of reference — introducing rhythmic, harmonic, and melodic elements from distant traditions. The listener discovers new cultural territory not through confrontation, but through organic transformation. A Warsaw waltz opens with echoes of Kabaret Starszych Panów and Agnieszka Osiecka, then unfolds into contemporary jazz grooves. A Polish-language song about the eternal wanderer weaves together Mariachi, Flamenco, and modern Israeli jazz. An Arabic-language composition is structured as a Western pop ballad. A traditional Turkish song is reimagined through Avishai Cohen–inspired jazz fusion in 5/8.

This methodology emerged not from theoretical design but from lived practice — from thousands of concerts across cultures, from spiritual traditions that underpin musical systems, and from the recognition that audiences can be physically moved by music yet mentally resist its implications if their cultural expectations are not first honoured.

#### Development and Planned Activities

*Kolot — The Voices: Home* is a 90-minute live concert programme comprising 23 pieces — original compositions and reimagined traditional material — in seven languages (Polish, English, Hebrew, Arabic, Turkish, Romani, and Italian, with elements of Ukrainian and Spanish), created by artists from seven countries: Israel, Germany, Poland, Hungary, the United States, Denmark, and Cuba.

The IKF coproduction supports the decisive next phase of this already tested programme: new arrangements through Warsaw-Berlin collaboration, expanded instrumentation, string quartet writing, rehearsal residencies, and cross-border presentations.

The development unfolds through a three-phase process. In the first phase, Gorelik and Dayan will develop new arrangements aligning the repertoire with Skrzypek's newly written lyrics — through refined harmonic language, string quartet scores, the integration of ethnic instruments (ney, oud, saz, bendir, riq) and ensemble choral parts, and the shortening of musical forms for greater accessibility without sacrificing substance. Studio sessions in Warsaw will serve as a creative laboratory for testing ensemble dynamics and refining the programme's dramatic arc. In the second phase, rehearsal residencies in Warsaw and Berlin will bring together the full ensemble to develop the concert programme as a cohesive performative experience. The third phase comprises presentations of the completed programme at venues in Poland and Germany, including the premiere at Centrum Kultury Jazovia in Gliwice, followed by presentations at Galeria Projekt (Warsaw), Teatr BOTO (Sopot), and Shlott (Berlin).

The lyrics — primarily by vocalist Agata Skrzypek — explore themes of belonging, displacement, and the search for home across languages and cultures. Gorelik contributes Hebrew texts, Cuban bassist Aniel Someillan writes in Spanish, and the programme incorporates traditional Turkish and Romani material. The interplay between familiar and unfamiliar languages mirrors the musical methodology: soft displacement operates simultaneously through sound and word.

### TEAM

Gorelik and Dayan first met as teenagers on the jam sessions of Israel's vibrant jazz scene, and Dayan soon joined Gorelik's ensemble. Together they worked alongside musicians who would go on to international prominence, including flutist Hadar Noiberg and bassist Or Bareket. Their creative partnership later deepened through the international ensemble KK Pearls, led by Polish pianist and composer Krzysztof Kobyliński, where they performed for several years alongside Trilok Gurtu, Louis Sclavis, and Zbigniew Namysłowski. This decades-long artistic foundation — built on deep listening, collective improvisation, and cross-cultural sensitivity — naturally led to the coproduction partnership at the heart of *Kolot*.

Tom Dayan, based in Berlin since 2009, is a composer and percussionist trained in classical composition at the Jerusalem Academy of Music and Dance (Prof. Michael Wolpe). Early in his development, he studied at the Matan summer workshop in Israel, where he worked with Arnie Lawrence and legendary contrabassist Richard Davis. A member of the Berlin Composers Orchestra and founder of the ensemble Mifrás, he brings a distinctive dual perspective — combining European classical training with jazz and improvised music practice. His role in *Kolot* is not limited to performance: as co-producer, he contributes arrangements, string quartet writing, and a compositional dialogue that deepens the programme's artistic architecture.

Vocalist Agata Skrzypek is a rare artistic presence: a Polish singer who performs professionally in eight languages, including Arabic and Hebrew, with training acquired through years of immersive study in over thirty countries — from the Sahara to Istanbul to the studios of Le Trio Joubran. Her ability to inhabit each language not merely phonetically but culturally is essential to the project's authenticity.

The ensemble further includes bassist Aniel Someillan (Cuba/Poland), a graduate of Havana's Amadeo Roldán Conservatory who has performed at the Montreux Jazz Festival; percussionist Haim Peskoff (Israel/Denmark), a recipient of Israel's "Outstanding Musician" distinction, whose father, guitarist Steve Peskoff, was a close friend of Arnie Lawrence — creating a generational continuity within the project's artistic lineage; and percussionist Peter Somos (Hungary), an internationally recognised specialist in both jazz drumming and Middle Eastern frame drum traditions. Legendary Polish bassist Krzysztof Ścierański regularly appears as a featured guest.

### RELEVANCE OF THE TOPIC AND IMPACT

The vision behind this project connects to a legacy that began in the 1990s, when Arnie Lawrence — invited to Jerusalem by Prime Minister Yitzhak Rabin — taught his students that patience and mutual respect are the foundation of any meaningful cultural exchange. Lawrence would sometimes stop a concert mid-performance, even before a thousand people, to speak about these values — a formative experience that reshaped Gorelik's understanding of what music can do. In an era of growing polarisation, this lesson remains urgently relevant. Yet *Kolot* goes beyond the familiar model of cultural fusion. Through two decades of performance across cultures, Gorelik has arrived at a deeper conviction: that every person already carries elements of a shared global cultural heritage, expressed through local traditions that differ on the surface but share common roots. The project's ambition is not to create a collage of cultures but to make this underlying unity audible — helping listeners recognise that the traditions they call their own are already part of a larger, shared musical heritage.

The methodology of *soft cultural displacement* translates this vision into artistic practice: the programme meets listeners within their own cultural comfort zone and gradually expands their frame of reference. Tested through a 10-concert tour across Poland in 2025, three music videos, and a broadcast on TVP Kultura, the approach demonstrates that audiences engage deeply when the entry point is culturally familiar.

While Poland — where jazz enjoys institutional support but cultural gatekeepers often resist elements perceived as foreign — serves as a compelling case study, the project's relevance extends far beyond any single national context. The question of how local cultures relate to a shared global heritage is equally pressing in Berlin, Jerusalem, Havana, and Budapest — the very cities from which the ensemble's musicians come.

The coproduction extends this dialogue to Berlin — a city sharing Warsaw's complex history of migration and reinvention. The exchange between a composer who left Israel for Poland and one who left for Germany embodies the project's central question: what does "home" mean when identity is woven from multiple traditions?

The project's anticipated impact is threefold: artistically, a fully developed concert programme with new arrangements, expanded instrumentation, and refined dramaturgy; organisationally, a durable Warsaw-Berlin coproduction partnership built on decades of shared practice; and publicly, a demonstration that intercultural music can function as natural artistic reality — as Lawrence's work in Jerusalem proved when Jewish-Arab musical collaboration went from utopia to axiom. A 5-minute film documentation will capture this creative process and its outcomes.
